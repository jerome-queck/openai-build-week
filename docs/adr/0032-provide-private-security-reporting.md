# Provide private security reporting

Clarifold will enable GitHub Private Vulnerability Reporting as the preferred channel for suspected vulnerabilities and will publish `security@jeromegroup.org` as a private fallback. A root `SECURITY.md` will identify the supported release policy, link to GitHub's private reporting form, provide the fallback address, request useful reproduction and impact details, and ask reporters not to disclose suspected vulnerabilities through public issues. Until Clarifold has a stable release, only the newest published beta build is supported for security fixes; older candidates, source snapshots, and modified forks carry no promise of a fix, although reports may still be considered. The support policy must be reconsidered for the first stable release. Conduct reports remain separate at `conduct@jeromegroup.org`.

`SECURITY.md` will call its testing boundary **Good-faith security testing**, not a generic research policy. It applies to people testing Clarifold code and builds they control with synthetic data; it does not authorize investigating Jerome Queck, accessing his accounts or devices, testing GitHub, OpenAI, Apple, or another third party, accessing another learner's data, social engineering, persistence, destructive or disruptive activity, or public disclosure of an unresolved vulnerability. Testers must collect only the minimum evidence required, report privately, stop and ask before continuing when scope is uncertain, and avoid retaining sensitive material.

Clarifold will treat testing that complies with the published boundary as authorized and will not initiate legal action over that testing. This limited statement creates no bug bounty or payment, response-time promise, immunity from third-party claims, or authority over third-party systems. Initial scope covers the repository and newest published beta only; any future hosted service must enumerate its own in-scope assets before testing. The wording must be reconsidered with Jerome Queck and should receive Singapore-appropriate legal review before operating a public hosted service or bug-bounty programme.
